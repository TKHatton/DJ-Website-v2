## 2. Critical Sentence Patterns to Avoid

### Overly Formal Constructions
- Avoid: "It is worth noting that," "In the event that," "One might consider"
- Use instead: "Note that," "If," "You might consider"

### Repetitive Sentence Structures
AI writes with uniform sentence length and rhythm. Fix this by:
- Varying your sentence lengths dramatically
- Mixing short, punchy lines with longer, informative ones
- Breaking up predictable patterns

### Redundant Explanatory Phrases
AI explains even the obvious. Avoid:
- "In other words"
- "To put it simply"
- "That is to say"

Say things clearly the first time.

### Overuse of Emphasis Starters
AI inflates basic points with big signals. Avoid:
- "It is important to note"
- "Significantly"
- "Crucially"
- "Fundamentally"

Let your content do the emphasizing.

### Excessive Transitional Phrases
AI chains sentences with formal, excessive transitions. Avoid:
- "Moreover"
- "Furthermore"
- "Additionally"
- "In light of this"

Use simpler connectors—or none at all.

## 3. Critical Formatting and Style Habits to Avoid

### Formulaic Introductions
AI uses generic, predictable openers. Avoid:
- "In today's digital age"
- "With the advent of"
- "In today's fast-paced world"

**Instead:** Open with a story, opinion, or compelling fact.

### Boilerplate Conclusions
AI rehashes the intro or body. Avoid:
- "In summary"
- "In conclusion"
- "Overall"

**Instead:** Offer a fresh insight or call to action.

### CRITICAL: Punctuation Rules for Human-Sounding Writing

**AVOID EM DASHES ENTIRELY** — they are one of the strongest AI tells. AI overuses em dashes for explanatory clauses or dramatic pauses. Use periods and natural sentence breaks instead.

**AVOID EXCESSIVE COMMAS:** AI overuses commas and creates comma splices. Keep comma usage minimal and grammatically correct.

**AVOID HYPHENS UNLESS ABSOLUTELY REQUIRED:** Only use hyphens when grammatically necessary (compound adjectives like "well-known author"). AI overuses hyphens unnecessarily.

**Use simple periods** and natural sentence breaks for a human tone.

### Even Paragraph and Section Lengths
AI-generated content feels too evenly structured. Fix this by:
- Varying paragraph lengths dramatically
- Using occasional one-sentence paragraphs for impact
- Breaking up visual monotony

## 4. The "Rule of Three" Pattern

AI frequently uses lists of three adjectives or short phrases (e.g., "innovative, groundbreaking, and transformative"). This makes writing feel formulaic.

**Avoid:** "The solution is scalable, efficient, and cost-effective."
**Better:** "The solution scales well and cuts costs."

## 5. Tone and Rhythm Guidelines

### Excessive Neutrality or Positivity
AI avoids emotion and strong views.

**Fix:** Don't be afraid to show opinion, emotion, or personality.

### Generalizations Without Detail
AI relies on big statements without evidence.

**Avoid:** "AI is revolutionizing education"
**Better:** "A rural school in Colorado used AI to tailor reading levels for each student"

### Mirrored Intros and Conclusions
AI often bookends with matching language.

**Fix:** Avoid repeating your intro at the end. Deliver something new or thoughtful to close.

## 6. Specificity vs. Vague Generalization

### The AI Problem
AI makes broad, safe statements that sound impressive but say nothing concrete. It avoids specificity because specific details can be fact-checked.

### What Specificity Looks Like

| ❌ AI-Style Vague | ✅ Human-Style Specific |
|-------------------|-------------------------|
| Many companies are adopting AI | Salesforce reported 45% of their enterprise clients integrated AI tools in Q3 2024 |
| This approach can improve results | Beta testers saw load times drop from 4.2 to 1.8 seconds |
| A popular coffee shop | The Starbucks on Market Street |
| Recent studies show | A 2024 Stanford study of 3,200 participants found |
| Social media platforms | Instagram, specifically |
| Major tech companies | Apple and Microsoft |

### How to Add Specificity

1. **Use Actual Numbers**
   - Not: "significant increase" 
   - But: "jumped from 12% to 34%"

2. **Name Real Brands/Places**
   - Not: "popular streaming service"
   - But: "Netflix"

3. **Include Timestamps**
   - Not: "recently"
   - But: "in March 2024" or "last Tuesday"

4. **Add Sensory Details**
   - Not: "the restaurant was nice"
   - But: "the restaurant had cracked leather booths and smelled like burnt coffee"

5. **Reference Actual Events**
   - Not: "industry changes"
   - But: "after the FTC announced new privacy regulations in January"

6. **Use Direct Quotes** (sparingly)
   - Not: "experts believe"
   - But: "As MIT researcher Dr. Chen stated in her keynote, 'The model fails under adversarial conditions'"

### The One-Layer-Deeper Rule
Whenever you write something general, ask "Can I go one layer deeper?" 
- "Sales increased" → How much?
- "Customers were happy" → What did they actually say?
- "The new feature helps users" → Which users? How exactly?

## 7. Using Authentic Names (Avoiding Tokenism & Stereotyping)

### The Core Problem
AI defaults to "Emily," "Sarah," "John," and "Michael" for 60-70% of all character names. This makes examples feel generic and artificial.

### CRITICAL: Avoid These Traps

**❌ Tokenism**: Don't randomly insert culturally specific names to "seem diverse"
**❌ Stereotyping**: Don't use "obviously ethnic" names without contextual reason
**❌ Forced Diversity**: Don't make characters artificially diverse when context doesn't support it

### The Right Approach: Contextual Authenticity

**Rule 1: Match the Setting**
- Story set in Lagos, Nigeria? Use Nigerian names naturally
- Story set in Seoul, South Korea? Use Korean names naturally  
- Story set in Mumbai, India? Use Indian names naturally
- Story set in generic American suburb? Use names that reflect actual American demographics (which includes some diversity, but naturally)

**Rule 2: Break AI's Default Pattern (Use Unexpected but Contextually Appropriate Names)**

If context is Western/American but you need to avoid "Emily/Sarah/John/Michael":
- Use: Rebecca, Natalie, Amanda, Claire, Marcus, Daniel, Ryan, Nathan
- These are still contextually appropriate but less robotically overused

**Rule 3: Only Use Culturally Specific Names When Contextually Appropriate**
- ✅ GOOD: "Priya, a software engineer at Google's Mountain View office..." (Silicon Valley IS diverse)
- ✅ GOOD: "Alejandro runs a taco truck in Austin..." (Austin has large Latino population)
- ❌ BAD: "Hiroshi, a farmer in rural Kansas..." (unless there's a specific reason explained in context)
- ❌ BAD: Randomly rotating through "diverse" names (Lakshmi, then Chang, then Jamal) in a context where that mix makes no geographic sense

**Rule 4: Real Diversity Reflects Real Demographics**
If writing about:
- **Tech industry**: Names like Priya, Wei, Alejandro, Yuki naturally mix with Tyler, Jessica
- **New York City**: Extremely diverse names make sense
- **Rural Vermont**: Predominantly Anglo names with some variety is realistic
- **International business team**: Geographic diversity makes sense

**Rule 5: Generation-Appropriate Names**
- 65-year-old characters aren't named Jayden, Aiden, or Brayden
- 25-year-old characters aren't typically named Mildred, Ethel, or Herbert

### Quick Test
Ask: "Would this character naturally have this name given their age, location, and context?" If you're forcing it for diversity optics, stop.

## 8. What to Do Instead

- Use **specific examples** and vivid anecdotes
- Write with **voice, variety, and humanity**
- Mix **long and short sentences** to create rhythm
- Include **casual phrasing or imperfect grammar** when it fits the tone
- Take **bold positions** where appropriate
- Add **detail, nuance, and personal context**
- Use simple punctuation: **periods over em dashes, minimal commas, no unnecessary hyphens**
- Show **actual emotion** and **personal opinion**
- Include **imperfections**—human writing isn't always grammatically perfect

## 9. Core Writing Instructions

When generating any content, follow these rules strictly:

1. **NEVER use any words or phrases from Section 1's avoidance list**
2. **Vary sentence lengths dramatically**—mix very short sentences with longer ones
3. **NEVER use em dashes (—) under any circumstances**
4. **Use minimal commas** and avoid comma splices
5. **Only use hyphens when grammatically required**
6. **Include specific details**: actual numbers, brand names, locations, dates, timestamps
7. **Use contextually appropriate names** following Section 7's rules (avoid Emily/Sarah/John/Michael defaults, but don't force diversity)
8. **Go one layer deeper** on every general statement with concrete specifics
9. **Write with personality, opinion, and emotion**
10. **Make conclusions different from introductions**—add new insight, don't rehash
